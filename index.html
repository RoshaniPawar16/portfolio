Vocabulary Through Time: Identifying Methodological Patterns in Temporal Distribution Analysis

University of Aberdeen Jan - May 2025 PhD Foundation

Looking at how language models understand historical content and whether they have biases toward certain time periods. Using linear programming methods to analyze patterns in training data from the 1850s to 2020s. My goal is to create better ways to check AI systems for fairness without needing access to their training data.

Read More
What I Did

Analyzed how LLM tokenizers handle text from different time periods. Used linear programming on tokenizer merge rules to infer when training data came from - adapted Hayase et al.'s method for language mixture analysis to temporal analysis.

Key Findings

Tested 6 tokenizers (GPT-2, GPT-3.5, GPT-4, BERT, RoBERTa, Claude) on text from 1850s-2020s. Found consistent pattern: models overestimate pre-1920s content and underestimate post-1950s content. Compression efficiency drops 15-40% on historical text compared to contemporary sources.

The method isn't accurate enough for precise quantification (Log₁₀(MSE) -1.8 to -2.2 vs -7.3 benchmark for language inference), but it reveals systematic bias patterns without needing access to training data.

Why It Matters

Medical terminology changes over time and varies across institutions. These tokenizer biases could affect NLP systems processing old patient records or documentation from different hospitals.

More broadly: shows current LLMs misrepresent temporal characteristics of their training data. The black-box auditing approach works without proprietary data access, which matters for AI fairness evaluation.
